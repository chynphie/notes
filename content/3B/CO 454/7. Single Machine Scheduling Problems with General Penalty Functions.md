#### Minimizing maximum Penalty (1/prec/$h_{max}$)

> [!info] Algorithm 32:
> 0. Let J denote the set of unscheduled jobs with no unscheduled successors $P:=\sum_{j=1}^{n}p_{j}$
> 1. Pick Job $j\in J$ s.t. $h_{1}(P)=min_{j\in J}\{ h_{j}(P) \}$
> 2. Schedule job l at the latest available time slot (i.e. So that $c_{l}=p$)
> 3. Set $p:p-p_{l}$, update $J$, go to Step 1

The objective function is 
$$
\begin{align}
 & \text{min}\:\sum_{j=1}^{n}w_{j}T_{j}\\
 & s.t.\:T_{j}\geq p_{j}-d_{j}+\sum_{i=1}^{n}p_{j}x_{ij}\:\forall j\in \{ 1,2,\dots,n \}
\end{align}
$$
> [!theorem] Theorem 33
> For every instances $1/prec/h_{max}$ where $h_j$ is non-decreasing for all j, then the Algorithm delivers the optimal schedule.

Proof : 
- <u>Base Case: </u> On the number of jobs in the given problem instance $n = 1$. There is only one feasible schedule (same as delivered by Algorithm 32).

- **<u>Inductive Hypothesis (IH):</u>**  Algo. 32 delivers optimal schedule for all instances of $1\text{prec}/h_{\max}$ with at most $n - 1$ jobs.
- **<u>Inductive Step (IS):</u>**  Consider all instances of $1\text{prec}/h_{\max}$ with $n$ jobs.
- **<u>Claim</u>:**  Let $j_n = \ell$ be the job scheduled by Algo. 32 as the last job in the schedule.  
  Then there exists an optimal schedule where job $\ell$ is last.

	- **Proof of claim by contradiction:**
	  - Assume there exists an instance of $1\text{prec}/h_{\max}$ for which Algo. 32 schedules job $\ell$ as the last job in the schedule.
	  - Also assume there exists an optimal schedule $S^*$ in which job $\ell$ is *not* the last job.
	
	- There exists an optimal schedule $S^*$ with the following structure:
	  - $S^*: \quad A \mid \ell \mid B \mid K \quad$ (consider)
	  - $S: \quad A \mid B \mid K \mid \ell \quad$ (Algo 32)
	
	- Note: Schedule $S'$ is feasible: $\ell$ and $K$ had no successors in the precedence graph since Algo. 32 scheduled job $\ell$ as last job over optimal schedule $S^* = (r_1, r_2, \dots, r_n)$.
	- Since $S'$ is derived from $S^*$, $S'$ satisfies all precedence constraints.
	
	- Let $j \in A \cup B \cup K \cup \{\ell\}$:
	  - $C_j(S') \leq C_j(S^*)$ since $h_j$ is non-decreasing
	  - $\forall j \in \{1, \dots, n\}, \ h_j(S') \leq h_j(S^*) \quad \forall j \in A \cup B \cup K$
	  - Also $h_\ell(S^*) > h_\ell(CS^*)$ and Algo. 32 considered both jobs $K$ and $\ell$, and chose job $\ell$ to schedule last
	  - $\Rightarrow h_{\max}(S) \in h_{\max}(CS^*)$ — a contradiction
	
	- Therefore:
		  - $\forall j \in A \cup B \cup K \cup \{\ell\}, \ h_j(S') \leq h_j(S^*) < h_{\max}^*$
		  - $h_\ell(S') < h_{\max}^*$
		- Let the $n-1$ job instance be the instance of $1\text{prec}/h_{\max}$ obtained by removing the data for job $\ell$ (scheduled by Algo. 32 as the last job).
		- Let $S'$ denote the schedule delivered by Algo. 32 on the reduced instance.
		- By the claim proved above, $h_{\max}(S') \leq h_{\max}^*$	
		- So, $h_{\max}(\bar{S}\: \text{for } (n-1)\text{-job instance}) \leq h_{\max}(\text{(n−1)-job instance}) \leq h_{\max}^*$



> [!theorem] Theorem 34
> For every instance of $1//F_w\equiv1//\sum w_{j}T_{j}$ for which every weight $w_{j}\geq0$, every optimal solution of the integer programming problem (IP-$T_{w}$) yields an optimal schedule 

Proof sketch:
Let $S$ be a feasible schedule for an arbitrary instance of $1||T_w$. Then we set  
$$
\bar{x}_{ij} =
\begin{cases}
1, & \text{if job } i \text{ comes before job } j \text{ in } S \\
0, & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\quad \forall \text{ distinct pairs } i, j \in \{1, 2, \dots, n\}
$$
$$
T_j = \max \left\{ 0,\ p_j - d_j + \sum_{\substack{i=1\\i \neq j}}^n p_i \bar{x}_{ij} \right\} \quad \forall j \in \{1, 2, \dots, n\}
$$
