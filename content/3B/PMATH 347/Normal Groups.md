If $\varphi: G \to H$ is a homomorphism, then $\ker \varphi \triangleleft G$.  

**Alternate proof:**  
Suppose $g \in G$ and we want to show $g\,(\ker \varphi)\,g^{-1} \subseteq \ker \varphi$.  If $x \in \ker \varphi$, then 
$$
\varphi\bigl(g x g^{-1}\bigr)
\;=\;\varphi(g)\,\varphi(x)\,\varphi(g)^{-1}
\;=\;\varphi(g)\,e_H\,\varphi(g)^{-1}
\;=\;e_H,
$$
so $g x g^{-1} \in \ker \varphi$.  Hence $g\,(\ker \varphi)\,g^{-1} \subseteq \ker \varphi$ for all $g$, and $\ker \varphi \triangleleft G$.  

---

>[!definition] **Definition (Normalizer).**  
> Let $S \subseteq G$.  The **normalizer** of $S$ in $G$ is  
> $$
> N_G(S)\;=\;\{\,g \in G : g\,S\,g^{-1} = S\,\}\;.  
> $$

---

>[!lemma]  
**Lemma 1.**  
$N_G(S)$ is a subgroup of $G$.  

**Proof:**  
1. $e_G \in N_G(S)$, since $e_G\,S\,e_G^{-1} = S$.  
2. If $g \in N_G(S)$, then $g\,S\,g^{-1} = S$.  For any $s \in S$, write $s = g\,s'\,g^{-1}$ for some $s' \in S$.  Then
   $$
   g^{-1}\,s\,g \;=\; g^{-1}\,(g\,s'\,g^{-1})\,g \;=\; s' \;\in\; S,
   $$
   so $g^{-1}\,S\,g = S$.  Hence $g^{-1} \in N_G(S)$.  
3. If $g_1,g_2 \in N_G(S)$, then $g_1\,S\,g_1^{-1} = S$ and $g_2\,S\,g_2^{-1} = S$.  Thus
   $$
   (g_1 g_2)\,S\,(g_1 g_2)^{-1}
   \;=\; g_1\,\bigl(g_2\,S\,g_2^{-1}\bigr)\,g_1^{-1}
   \;=\; g_1\,S\,g_1^{-1}
   \;=\; S,
   $$
   so $g_1 g_2 \in N_G(S)$.  

Therefore $N_G(S) \le G$.  $\quad \blacksquare$  

---

>[!lemma]  **Lemma 2.**  
> Let $H \le G$.  Then $H \subseteq N_G(H)$, and 
> $$
> H \triangleleft G \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad N_G(H) \;=\; G.
> $$

**Proof (sketch):**  
- If $h\in H$, then $h\,H\,h^{-1} = H$ because $H$ is a subgroup.  Hence $h \in N_G(H)$, so $H \subseteq N_G(H)$.  
- By definition, $H \triangleleft G$ means $g\,H\,g^{-1} = H$ for all $g\in G$.  But that is exactly the condition “$g \in N_G(H)$ for all $g\in G$,” i.e.\ $N_G(H)=G$.  

$\quad \blacksquare$  

>_Note:_ Normal subgroups are not necessarily transitive.  For example, if $H \triangleleft N \triangleleft G$, it does *not* force $H \triangleleft G$.  

---

>[!corollary]  
>Suppose $G = \langle S\rangle$ is generated by a subset $S$, and $H \le G$.  Then  
> $$
> H \triangleleft G 
> \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
> g\,H\,g^{-1} = H \;\text{ for all }\; g \in S 
> \;\Longleftrightarrow\; 
> g\,H = H\,g \;\text{ for all }\; g \in S.
> $$

**Proof (outline):**  
- If $g\,H\,g^{-1} = H$ for every generator $g \in S$, then any product of generators also normalizes $H$.  Hence $g\,H\,g^{-1}=H$ for all $g\in G$, so $H\triangleleft G$.  
- Conversely, if $H\triangleleft G$ then $gHg^{-1}=H$ for all $g\in G$, and in particular for all $g\in S$.  Finally, $gHg^{-1}=H \iff gH=Hg$ because these sets have the same size.  

$\quad \blacksquare$  

---

>[!lemma]  **Lemma 3.**  
Let $S \le G$ be a subgroup of finite index, i.e.\ $[G : S] < \infty$.  If $g\,S\,g^{-1} = S$, then $g\,S = S\,g$.  

**Proof:**  
Since $[G:S] < \infty$, the cosets of $S$ in $G$ are finite in number.  Now, if $g\,S\,g^{-1} = S$, then for each $s \in S$,
$$
(g\,s)\,S
\;=\;g\,(\underbrace{s\,g^{-1})\,S}_{=\,S\,g^{-1}} 
\;=\;g\,S\,g^{-1}\,S
\;=\;S\,S \;=\; S,
$$
so $g\,s \in S\,(\text{something})$.  One shows more systematically that 
$$
g\,S = \bigl\{\,g\,s : s \in S\bigr\}
$$ 
and 
$$
S\,g = \bigl\{\,s'\,g : s' \in S\bigr\}
$$ 
are two cosets of $S$ in $G$, and any two cosets of $S$ either coincide or are disjoint.  But because $g\,S\,g^{-1}=S$, the coset $g\,S$ must land in the same “rack” of cosets as $S\,g$, forcing $g\,S = S\,g$.  

$\quad \blacksquare$  

---

>[!corollary]  **Corollary 3.1.**  
> If $G$ is finite and $H \le G$, then  
> $$
> N_G(H) \;=\; \{\,g \in G : g\,H\,g^{-1} = H\,\} 
> \;=\; \{\,g \in G : g\,H = H\,g\}.
> $$

**Proof:**  
For finite $G$, $g\,H\,g^{-1} = H \iff g\,H = H\,g$ by the previous lemma (because $H$ has finite index in itself and cosets coincide exactly when conjugation preserves $H$).  

$\quad \blacksquare$  

---

>[!corollary]  **Corollary 3.2.**  
> Suppose $G$ is finite and cyclic: $G = \langle g \rangle$.  Let $H \le G$.  Then  
> $$
> H \triangleleft G \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad g^k \in H \;\text{for all}\; k \text{ such that }g^k\in H.
> $$

>**Warning:** This equivalence can fail if $G$ is not finite.
